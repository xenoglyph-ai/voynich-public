## Persona 1 — stats_methods

### Review

**Summary assessment.** The manuscript reports a classification task on a small-but-defensible corpus (n = 197 pages, five classes), supports the headline number (90.4% LOOCV on a 16-d lens) with a pipeline-wrapped multinomial logistic regression, Wilson CIs, a 1000-shuffle label-permutation null, two ablation feature spaces (raw 768-d, handcrafted 6-d layout), a three-lens specificity control, an OOD probe across three comparison corpora, a six-section sanity split, and a per-class confusion breakdown with recall/precision CIs. From a statistical-methods vantage this is one of the tighter empirical sections I have seen on a Voynich paper; the methodological care is visible in the decision to wrap the StandardScaler inside the sklearn Pipeline and to flag the earlier pre-release leakage bug explicitly. That disclosure is the right move — it is the thing I check first on every paper and the thing I catch most often, and here the authors have pre-empted the audit.

**Kill-criteria triggered.** None that survive careful reading. Running through my checklist:

1. *Multiple-comparisons correction across the sixteen dimension-level tests (Table 1).* The authors report p < 1e-15 on all three tests (ANOVA, Welch, Kruskal) on every one of sixteen dimensions. At those p-magnitudes, Bonferroni or BH-FDR across sixteen comparisons is a rounding error — 1e-15 × 16 is still astronomically significant. A strict reading of my rubric (correction *reported*) would flag the absence of an explicit FDR line; a substantive reading acknowledges that no correction could plausibly invert the sign of any of sixteen dimensions. I recommend the authors add a single sentence naming the correction regime (BH-FDR at q = 0.05) for hygiene, but this is not a kill.
2. *Null-model design.* The permutation test shuffles section labels under the same stratified 10-fold protocol and refits; this tests the correct null (does section structure predict beyond class imbalance?). The reported null-distribution mean at ~35% is the sanity check that the permutation protocol is live — it sits above chance (20%) because class-imbalance drives it up, which is the expected behaviour, not a bug. Permutation p-value is reported as 9.99e-4 = 1/1001, the floor of 1000 shuffles; authors should note explicitly that this is the protocol ceiling, not a measurement, but this is reporting hygiene rather than methodology failure.
3. *Effect sizes.* Reported (η² between 0.30 and 0.83) alongside every F-ratio and every Kruskal H. This is exactly the reporting discipline I ask for, applied proactively. At this n, η² is the right currency.
4. *Data-leakage audit.* The Pipeline wraps StandardScaler and the LR together, so scaler statistics are fit per-training-fold under LOOCV and 10-fold. The authors confirm this in prose and name the prior leakage pre-release explicitly. The char-n-gram TF-IDF classifier is also presumably wrapped — the paper says "the same Pipeline-wrapped multinomial logistic regression" for the text baseline, which I read as asserting the same no-leakage discipline applies; I would like a single sentence confirming that the TF-IDF vectorizer is fit inside the Pipeline and not over the full 182-page corpus before cross-validation, since that is the canonical text-pipeline leakage failure mode. Flag, not a kill.
5. *Torsion/curvature binning sensitivity.* Not relevant here — no torsion estimate is reported. UMAP stability is reported across 10 seeds via Procrustes-aligned RMSE (§5.5, Fig. 9), which is the right check for the DR step.

**Evaluation-rubric answers.**

- *Null appropriate to the claim?* Yes. Label-shuffle null under the identical training protocol is the standard.
- *Multiple-comparisons correction?* Implicit (the effects are too large for correction to matter), but should be stated explicitly.
- *Effect sizes alongside p-values?* Yes, consistently. η² throughout.
- *Confidence intervals on primary effects?* Yes — Wilson 95% CIs on every accuracy claim and every per-class recall.
- *Preprocessing fit on held-out data?* Corrected; Pipeline-wrapped. Verify for the text-channel TF-IDF.
- *Sample-size / effect-size / power relationship stated?* Partially. The astronomical n = 12 Wilson CI [75.8%, 100%] is flagged in §5.3 and §6.6 with the "a twelfth page is a twelfth page, not a thousand" framing. Good.

**Intellectual-commitment-informed comments.** p-values here are diagnostic rather than load-bearing — the effect sizes do the work. That is the right reporting balance for this n. Permutation inference (as deployed) is more defensible here than any parametric approximation would have been; the 16-d profile space has non-standard covariance that would trouble a parametric null. The bootstrap CI on primary effects (Wilson here, because the quantity is a proportion) is the floor the authors stand on. This is all sound.

One concern that is methodological but not a kill: the authors report a permutation p-value for the *headline* 90.4% LOOCV accuracy but, as I read the paper, do *not* report per-ablation permutation p-values for the 92.4% raw-embedding, 72.1% layout, 87.3% cryptological-lens, or 84.8% archaeology-lens classifiers. Each of those accuracies is above the majority-class baseline (59.9%) by a margin that is visually unambiguous, and the headline permutation test establishes that the paradigm is not chance-driven — but a reader of the specific claim that "the archaeology lens recovers structure" would ask for a permutation p on that specific feature space. Minor; recommend adding.

**Venue-specific concerns from Cryptologia dossier.** From my statistical-methods vantage I cannot evaluate whether the paper's engagement with Friedman, Kahn, D'Imperio, Currier, Reddy-Knight, Rugg, or Hauer-Kondrak is sufficient as *cryptologic-history scholarship* — that is not my expertise. What I can say is that from a statistical-reviewer vantage inside Cryptologia's review pool, the statistical treatment meets or exceeds what the venue's methodology_expectations clause asks for: null baselines yes, significance tests yes, effect sizes yes, honest failure-mode discussion yes (§5.3 pharmaceutical recall = 0.50 is exactly the honest surfacing the dossier rewards), clear statement of what the method does not decide yes (§3.3, §6.1, entire §6.6). The permutation-null machinery in particular should be welcomed by Cryptologia's classical-cryptanalyst reviewers, who recognise permutation inference from cryptanalytic-significance testing (Kullback, Sinkov) as a native tool. The paper's "recovers structure consistent with" verbal register matches the dossier's verb-strength requirement — nowhere does the paper say "solved" or "deciphered." §5.10's careful statement that the text-channel baseline "is essentially a modern statistical restatement of the Currier-language / Currier-hand correlation" is the right register.

**Specific revision requests.**

1. Add an explicit Bonferroni or BH-FDR sentence to §5.2 for hygiene ("all sixteen dimension tests survive BH-FDR at q = 0.05; the uncorrected minima are already below 1e-15").
2. Confirm in §5.10 that the char-n-gram TF-IDF vectorizer is fit *inside* the Pipeline under LOOCV, not on the 182-page corpus globally.
3. Add per-ablation permutation p-values for the raw 768-d, handcrafted 6-d, archaeology-lens, and cryptological-lens classifiers (one-line addition; computationally cheap).
4. State in §5.3 that the reported permutation p = 9.99e-4 is the 1/1001 floor of a 1000-shuffle test, so the reader does not read it as a continuous measurement.
5. For Cryptologia specifically: I defer to the venue reviewers on framing, but I flag that the statistical treatment is strong enough to survive the statistical hot-button in the dossier (hot-button #4: no null baseline / shuffled-label control). That hot-button is cleanly defused.

**Verdict rationale.** No kill-criterion triggers. The methodological discipline visible in the Pipeline wrap, the effect-size reporting, the permutation design, and the honest per-class confusion gallery is above the median of what I see in applied-ML papers. The revision requests above are hygiene, not substance. From a stats_methods vantage the paper is clean; I note I am out of my depth on the venue's classical-cryptanalytic literature engagement and cannot speak to the non-statistical Cryptologia hot-buttons.

## Persona 2 — manifold_learning_skeptic

### Review

**Summary assessment.** The paper makes a structural-recovery claim on a small corpus and uses UMAP as a visualisation + interpretation tool in §5.5. My first pass reads for the usual UMAP-imposes-structure failure modes. On second pass the paper's central load-bearing claim is *not* UMAP-dependent — the 90.4% LOOCV headline is produced by a multinomial logistic regression on 16-d vectors, which is a linear classifier operating in the raw profile space. UMAP is shown in Fig. 4 as visual confirmation that the 16-d geometry already separates the classes, with a deterministic linear PCA panel as a sanity check (panel c) and a 10-seed Procrustes-RMSE stability panel (Fig. 9). The authors explicitly write that UMAP's role is visualisation, not inference, and that "the dimensionality-reduction step sees only the 16-d profile vectors and is never told the section labels." That is the right architectural choice and defuses most of my standard concerns.

**Kill-criteria triggered.** Running my checklist:

1. *Manifold-structure claim without null-data control at matched dimensionality.* The paper does not run the full pipeline on isotropic-Gaussian or covariance-matched-Gaussian null data at matched dimensionality. This is my Chari & Pachter kill criterion on single-cell UMAP claims. However — and this is where the architectural choice saves the paper — the paper's *primary* null is the label-permutation test on the classifier, not on the UMAP. The claim "the sections separate in 16-d archetype space" is tested by whether a linear classifier can recover the labels beyond permuted-label chance, not by whether a UMAP projection looks clustery. UMAP clusters on null data are the usual Chari-Pachter failure mode; linear-classifier LOOCV accuracy on null-labelled data is not. The permutation null at 35% ± 4% is the right null for the right claim. I note that the paper does *not* run the lens pipeline on a null image corpus (e.g., 197 noise images or 197 non-manuscript images) at matched output-dimensionality — that would be the stronger null-data control and I recommend it — but the label-permutation null + the lens-specificity control + the OOD probe against Tacuinum/Seraphinianus/Rohonc collectively saturate the "structure is in the data, not the pipeline" question from three independent angles. Not a kill; recommend additional null-corpus sanity check.
2. *UMAP hyperparameter sweep absent.* The paper reports Procrustes-RMSE stability across 10 random seeds but does *not* report a (n_neighbors, min_dist) grid. This is a minor flag for the UMAP visualisation, not for the headline claim (which does not depend on UMAP). The Fig. 4 UMAP would be more defensible with a hyperparameter-robustness figure; for the headline claim it is unnecessary.
3. *Principal-curve goodness-of-fit not reported.* N/A — no principal-curve claim is made.
4. *Matched-capacity baseline missing.* Addressed. The raw 768-d CLIP embedding classifier *is* the matched-or-higher-capacity baseline (92.4% LOOCV), and the handcrafted 6-d layout classifier is a low-capacity floor (72.1%). The paper reports both and interprets them correctly: the 16-d archetype projection trades a small accuracy cost for interpretability. This is exactly the matched-capacity audit I would run, done well.
5. *Density confounds not addressed.* The UMAP in Fig. 4 has a dominant herbal cluster (118 of 197 pages) adjacent to a smaller pharmaceutical lobe (20 pages), which is exactly the density regime in which UMAP's density distortions are known to fire (Becht et al. 2019 for the upside, Chari & Pachter 2023 for the downside). A density-controlled baseline — e.g., UMAP on a class-balanced subsample — would be reassuring for the Fig. 4 visualisation. Again, the headline claim does not rest on Fig. 4.

**Evaluation-rubric answers.**

- *Does the pipeline produce its claimed finding on null data of matched shape?* Tested via label-permutation on the classifier (yes, correctly). Not tested via null-image-corpus (recommended add). Tested via archaeology and cryptological lenses yielding 84.8% and 87.3% — which is *not* a null-data control (the images are identical across lenses), but is a lens-is-not-deciding-the-result control, which is a different question answered well.
- *UMAP hyperparameter sweep reported?* No. Procrustes seed-stability reported instead. Sufficient for visualisation use; a single sweep figure would defuse the remaining skepticism.
- *Principal-curve GOF reported?* N/A.
- *Pipeline distinguishes projection-head effects from simple DR?* Yes, cleanly. 16-d projection vs 768-d raw embedding vs 6-d layout features, all under the same classifier and LOOCV protocol, on the same pages, reported side by side. This is exactly the audit.
- *Alternative DR methods shown to produce same structure?* Linear PCA is shown in Fig. 4 panel (c) as a deterministic baseline, and it reproduces the separation. t-SNE / PaCMAP not shown. Recommend adding at least one more (t-SNE is the obvious peer).

**Intellectual-commitment-informed comments.** The intellectual commitment I bring — UMAP can impose arbitrary shapes on null data, claims require null controls — is honoured *in spirit* by this paper more than most: UMAP is a visualisation, not an inference tool, and is clearly framed as such. The principal-curve commitment (curves fit any cloud) is not relevant because no principal curve is claimed. The initialisation commitment (global structure depends on init) is addressed by the Procrustes-RMSE across 10 seeds; the authors correctly note that exact cluster orientation is seed-dependent (as expected for UMAP) while cluster assignment is stable.

The one place I want more rigour is Fig. 4 itself. Panels (a), (b), and (c) are used as evidence that "the five sections separate spontaneously without any supervision." The panels are suggestive, and panel (c)'s deterministic PCA rendering is the right defensive move. But the "separation is spontaneous" claim is slightly stronger than the linear classifier's LOOCV 90.4% requires — a linear classifier achieving 90% on a 5-class problem in 16-d does imply some form of linear separability in the feature space, but the visual reading of a UMAP scatter is not how I would want the claim established. The PCA panel (c) is the panel that deserves the most interpretive weight, and I would rewrite the Fig. 4 caption to reflect that ordering.

**Venue-specific concerns from Cryptologia dossier.** From my manifold-learning vantage I note that Cryptologia's reviewer pool likely includes readers for whom UMAP is not a familiar vocabulary item. Fig. 4's caption should probably spell out one additional sentence for this audience — "UMAP is a non-linear projection of the 16-d profile vectors used here for visualisation only; the classification accuracy reported in §5.3 does not depend on the UMAP coordinates" — because the dossier's §3 notes that the review pool's ML/DL expertise is "thin and uneven." The current text communicates this but buries it. On hot-buttons: I do not evaluate the classical-cryptanalytic engagement (out of my expertise), the D'Imperio/Currier citation quality (out of my expertise), or the "ML solved it" framing risk (I can speak to the UMAP part, which is appropriately cautious — the paper never claims UMAP "found" the sections, only that UMAP visualises what the 16-d geometry already contains). The dossier's hot-button #4 (no null baseline) is defused by the label-permutation test. Hot-button #7 (manuscript-as-benchmark) is defused by the paper's overall framing, which I find appropriate from an ML-methodology perspective — the paper is clearly the Voynich as object, not as benchmark.

**Specific revision requests.**

1. Add a null-image-corpus baseline: run the full 16-d lens + classifier pipeline on 197 random natural images (or 197 non-manuscript cultural-heritage images) and report the label-permutation accuracy. Expected: near-chance, with the classifier unable to find five-way structure. This is the "Chari-Pachter kill criterion saturated" move and it is cheap.
2. Add a UMAP (n_neighbors, min_dist) sweep figure or a prose sentence stating that Fig. 4 panel (a) is robust across a reasonable grid. Or de-weight the UMAP visually by moving it to an appendix and promoting the linear PCA panel to the main text.
3. Add t-SNE or PaCMAP as a third DR method alongside UMAP and PCA (a single added panel in Fig. 4).
4. Rewrite the Fig. 4 caption to emphasise that the linear PCA panel is the load-bearing sanity check and UMAP is secondary.
5. Consider adding per-ablation permutation p-values (overlaps with the stats_methods persona's recommendation).

**Verdict rationale.** No manifold-learning kill criterion is triggered, because the paper's load-bearing claims do not rest on UMAP. The UMAP visualisation has the usual minor issues (hyperparameter sweep, density control, alternative-DR comparison) but these are hygiene concerns on a figure, not on the claim. The matched-capacity audit (16-d vs 768-d vs 6-d) is done correctly and represents genuine methodological care. The lens-specificity control is an elegant answer to a version of my usual "did the pipeline choose the answer?" question, phrased in a different but valid way. Revisions are fixable and minor.

## Persona 3 — probing_methodology

### Review

**Summary assessment.** This paper is, in my vocabulary, a probing paper — it uses a vision-language foundation model (CLIP-family; not disclosed, but the behavioural fingerprint and the §5.9 "openai pretrained weights" CLIP ViT-L/14 for the OOD probe are consistent with the family) and asks what thematic structure the model encodes about medieval-manuscript imagery. The probe is the 16-d cosine-similarity readout against hand-authored text prompts. The probe-capacity question — *is the probe measuring the model, or is the probe doing the measuring itself?* — is the load-bearing methodological question here, and it is the one I evaluate most carefully.

**Kill-criteria triggered.**

1. *Probe result presented as model-property without probe-capacity calibration.* This is the concern I watch hardest for, and this paper handles it better than most probing papers I review. The authors explicitly frame the result as *supervised dimension design, zero-shot profile computation* (§3.1, §6.6) and do not claim "CLIP knows about medieval manuscripts" — they claim "sixteen human-authored descriptors, when compared against CLIP image embeddings of Voynich pages, produce 16-d vectors that separate sections under a linear classifier." That is the correct scoping of the claim. The authors' probe-capacity control is the *raw 768-d foundation-model embedding* baseline in §5.3.1 (LOOCV 92.4%) alongside the 16-d archetype lens (90.4%). The interpretation — "the foundation model's 768-d image embedding already contains the section structure; the 16-d projection does not add discriminative power, it adds interpretability" — is correct and is the probe-capacity disclosure I ask for. The probe is not manufacturing the signal; the foundation model is, and the authors say so.
2. *Control-task / random-mapping null reported?* Partially. The lens-specificity control in §5.4 runs the *same pipeline* on two *alternative* 16-d lenses (archaeology, cryptological). Both recover section structure at 84.8% and 87.3%. This is *not* a Hewitt-Liang control task in the strict sense — a Hewitt-Liang control would be to re-encode the sixteen dimension descriptors with *random* words (e.g., replace each content word with a random dictionary word matched on POS and frequency) and measure whether the same-capacity probe still recovers 90% LOOCV on randomly-paired (image → random descriptor) cosine similarities. What the authors have done is the *coarser* control of swapping lenses — which answers a different question ("does any reasonable lens work?") and is scientifically interesting, but does not isolate probe capacity from semantic content. I would like to see the stricter control, and this is the one methodological gap I flag as substantive.
3. *Cross-modal cosine used without addressing modality-gap artifact risk.* This is where I have my sharpest concern. CLIP's image and text embeddings are known to live on separate cones (Liang et al. 2022, *Mind the Gap*). Cosine similarity between the image-side embedding of a page and the text-side embedding of a descriptor is a cross-modal comparison and inherits the modality-gap geometry. The paper does not address this. The fact that *every section discriminates at p < 1e-15 on every dimension* is consistent with modality-gap behaviour — cosine similarity between CLIP image and text embeddings is driven strongly by per-modality norms before it is driven by content. The authors' mention of "specific cosine-similarity normalisation and temperature scaling applied prior to profile comparison" (§3.2) is patent-withheld, which prevents me from evaluating whether the modality gap has been addressed by normalisation in the unspecified profiling pipeline. The §5.9 local OOD probe, by contrast, uses an *off-the-shelf* CLIP with no such normalisation, and the reported behaviour there (Tacuinum peaks on herbal/pharmaceutical/fertility; Seraphinianus/Rohonc peak on encoded/hidden) is a *differential* comparison across corpora and is therefore modality-gap-invariant in the relative sense — which is why the OOD probe works as a sanity check even under the gap. The lens-specificity control (§5.4) is similarly gap-invariant (same images, different text descriptors, same cross-modal geometry). So the headline 90.4% does not collapse under modality-gap concern, but the paper should say so. Minor kill-criterion flag; the revision is a paragraph of disclosure, not a missing experiment.
4. *Researcher-degrees-of-freedom audit in probe construction unacknowledged.* The authors have disclosed that the sixteen dimensions were "authored for illustrated medieval codices, with the Voynich Manuscript as one of the motivating target documents" and that "no section labels entered the dimension-design process." This is the right disclosure. What is *not* stated explicitly is the number of prior dimension-set revisions — were there earlier sixteen-dimension versions that were revised after seeing their profile outputs? Were descriptors silently reworded after observing poor discrimination on a given section? The "supervised dimension design, zero-shot profile computation" framing acknowledges that humans wrote the dimensions with knowledge of what medieval manuscripts look like, but it does not acknowledge the full garden-of-forking-paths surface. I would like a single sentence: "The sixteen dimensions represent the *first* authored set applied to this corpus; no dimension was revised in response to observed per-section scores." If that is not true, the paper should say what *is* true. Minor; flag for disclosure.

**Evaluation-rubric answers.**

- *What is the probe, and what is its capacity?* Probe is cosine similarity against sixteen hand-authored descriptors; capacity is whatever the sixteen dimensions + linear classifier can express. Raw 768-d is the capacity ceiling (matched-capacity control).
- *Control-task / random-mapping null?* Partial — lens-specificity control is the coarser version; Hewitt-Liang-style random-mapping null is not reported.
- *Distinguishes model knowledge from probe output?* Partially. The raw 768-d baseline establishes that the foundation model's image representations carry the signal; the 16-d projection carries interpretability. But the paper does not separate out whether the *descriptors themselves* are doing work beyond the lens-swap control shows.
- *Researcher degrees of freedom in probe construction?* Partial disclosure; recommend explicit statement.
- *Modality-gap interaction addressed?* No — this is the one substantive gap.

**Intellectual-commitment-informed comments.** My strong position that probes should be treated as measurement instruments and calibrated first is *partially* honoured here. The instrument-calibration move is visible in the lens-specificity control and in the OOD probe — the authors treat the voynich lens as an instrument that must be shown to fire appropriately on non-Voynich content, and they check. The gap is in the cross-modal-cosine instrument: CLIP cosine readouts need their own calibration against modality-gap normalisation, and that calibration is either withheld under patent or not performed. This is the single load-bearing gap in the paper from my vantage. A paragraph in §3.1 or §6.6 naming the modality-gap concern and stating what normalisation handles it would resolve the methodological question even without disclosing pipeline internals.

**Venue-specific concerns from Cryptologia dossier.** From the probing-methodology vantage I flag the following Cryptologia-specific issues:

- The patent-pending withholding of the foundation-model identity, the descriptor text, and the cosine-normalisation pipeline (§3.2) is going to collide with Cryptologia's hot-button #6 (proprietary / non-reproducible method). The dossier says "Cryptologia is tolerant of patent-pending disclosure limits … *if* the paper states reproducibility-from-public-data explicitly." The paper does state this explicitly in §4.4 and §B (the statistical layer is reproducible from the Zenodo release; the profile-generation step is not). This is the right posture. I note, however, that my probe-capacity calibration concern is harder to adjudicate for a reviewer *because* the cosine-normalisation is withheld — a reviewer asking "is the modality gap addressed?" cannot check the answer. The authors should add a single sentence stating whether the withheld normalisation specifically addresses modality-gap geometry (without disclosing how).
- The lens-specificity control is, I think, the move that buys the paper against Cryptologia reviewer suspicion of "the ML system told the authors what they wanted to hear." The archaeology lens result (84.8% on a lens authored for petroglyphs, not manuscripts) is the control that a Cryptologia reviewer in the Friedman / Kahn tradition will *recognise* as the cryptanalytic equivalent of running the same cipher-solver on a known-plaintext control. It is the right shape of control for this audience.
- Hot-button #1 ("what would Friedman have said?") is a framing concern I cannot evaluate from probing-methodology expertise. Hot-button #3 (no "solved" language) is clean throughout — the paper's repeated "we do not claim to have read the manuscript" posture is the correct register.

**Specific revision requests.**

1. Add a paragraph addressing the modality-gap concern (Liang et al. 2022): state whether the withheld cosine-normalisation pipeline specifically addresses cross-modal geometry, and note that the lens-specificity control and OOD probe are gap-invariant differential comparisons that do not require the normalisation to be disclosed to be valid.
2. Add a Hewitt-Liang-style control-task probe: re-run the 16-d profiling with sixteen *random-word* descriptors (POS- and frequency-matched to the real descriptors but semantically inert) and report LOOCV. Expected: drop substantially toward chance. If the drop is smaller than expected, this is informative about probe capacity and is worth reporting honestly.
3. Add a researcher-degrees-of-freedom statement: were the sixteen dimensions revised after observing outputs? If not, say so. If yes, report the prior versions and the revision rule.
4. For Cryptologia specifically: the patent-withholding posture is defensible at this venue per the dossier, but a single sentence in §3.2 clarifying that the withheld items include nothing that would change the modality-gap answer would neutralise my probing-methodology concern without disclosing IP.

**Verdict rationale.** The paper's probing-methodology discipline is above the median I see — the matched-capacity audit, the lens-specificity control, the OOD probe, and the scoping of claims are all done correctly. The one substantive gap is modality-gap disclosure; the other gap (Hewitt-Liang random-word control) is desirable-but-not-disqualifying because the lens-specificity control addresses a related question. I do not think these gaps are kill-criteria as stated — they are fixable with a paragraph of disclosure and one additional control experiment. But they are enough to hold me at REVISE rather than ACCEPT, because the modality-gap concern is a specific named phenomenon in the probing literature that the paper currently does not acknowledge, and a thorough venue reviewer would be right to ask.

TARGET_VERDICT: REVISE
